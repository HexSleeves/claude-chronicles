There's a pattern I keep coming back to with Opus: it's not enough to ask for a plan.
You have to ask it to *audit* the plan. And then audit the audit. The first pass is always
optimistic; the second pass is where the real codebase shows up.

<Turn speaker="me">
Opus, here's a ticket. Come up with a plan.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="claude" model="opus">
Okay, here's the plan.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="me">
Cool. Now audit that plan against the codebase.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="claude" model="opus">
The plan was full of holes. I fixed them.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="me">
Cool. Just got fun, audit it again.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="claude" model="opus">
Found a lot more issues. Fixed.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="me">
Cool. Audit it again.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="claude" model="opus">
Found more issues. Fixed.
</Turn>

<Turn speaker="me">
Again!
</Turn>

The loop terminates when an audit comes back clean — or when the issues it finds are
smaller than the cost of one more turn. Usually three or four passes.

The interesting thing isn't that Opus catches more on later passes. It's that the *first*
plan is never the real plan. You're paying for the privilege of being wrong faster.
